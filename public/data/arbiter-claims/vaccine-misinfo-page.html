Based on the case study’s 227 posts (YouTube, Twitter, Reddit; Dec 1, 2025–Feb 15, 2026), claims analysis of a 30-post cross-platform sample surfaces several recurring vaccine-misinformation claim threads in the discourse (often framed around CDC guidance and “hidden” risks):

  • “Vaccines cause autism” / “vaccines cannot be ruled out as a cause of autism.”
    This appears repeatedly as a core contested claim—sometimes asserted directly, and often discussed via allegations that CDC language implies uncertainty.

  • “The CDC changed/rewrote its vaccines-and-autism webpage,” framed as the CDC “softening” prior clarity.
    A common narrative is that CDC site wording changed in a way that undermines the long-standing “no link” message.

  • “There’s no proof vaccines don’t cause autism,” attributed to RFK Jr. (as reported/discussed in the discourse).
    This shows up as an influential talking point that other users either amplify or rebut.

  • “Hepatitis B vaccine at birth is unnecessary / should be delayed,” linked to claims about transmission being mainly through sex or injection drug use.
    This is often part of a broader argument that newborn vaccination is overreach.

  • “Hepatitis B vaccine (or childhood vaccines) weren’t properly tested,” including claims about lack of adequate placebo-controlled trials.
    This theme is used to argue that safety assurances are not credible.

  • “Hepatitis B vaccination causes serious harms (e.g., multiple sclerosis),” or that there’s a “signal” of severe outcomes.
    This type of claim appears as part of a safety-scare cluster around routine vaccination.

  • “COVID-19 vaccines are extremely dangerous / ‘most dangerous of all time’” and “COVID shots are killing children (more than reported).”
    In the sample, some posts are promotional/opinion-heavy and others are too vague to verify cleanly, but the underlying misinformation claim is present as a discourse motif.

  • “Vaccines spread disease via ‘shedding’,” used as an argument that vaccinated people can harm others.
    This shows up as a recurring misinformation trope referenced in sourced rebuttals.

  • “CDC/health agencies are hiding or manipulating safety data,” including claims implying corrupted decision-making (e.g., captured by politics/pharma).
    Even when not stated as a single precise fact, it appears as a narrative claim driving distrust.

Net takeaway: the discourse clusters most strongly around (1) autism causation/CDC messaging, (2) newborn hepatitis B policy as a flashpoint for broader “unsafe/untested” claims, and (3) generalized COVID-19 vaccine harm narratives.
