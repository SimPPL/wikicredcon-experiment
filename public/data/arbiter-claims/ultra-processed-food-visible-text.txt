Based on the case study’s 517 posts (YouTube, Twitter, Reddit; Dec 1, 2025–Feb 1, 2026), claims analysis of a 30-post sample from the ultra‑processed food discourse surfaced several recurring claim clusters (87 distinct claim statements extracted in that sample):

Definition/identification claims

Ultra‑processed foods are industrial formulations heavily altered from original foods, often using ingredients “not found in a home kitchen.”
Ultra‑processed foods commonly contain additives such as preservatives, emulsifiers, sweeteners, flavor enhancers, colorants, and modified starches.
Example lists used to label foods as ultra‑processed: packaged snacks, instant noodles/soups, sugary cereals, soft drinks, fast food, frozen meals, flavored yogurts/sauces, reconstituted meats (e.g., nuggets/deli meats).
NOVA is referenced as the classification framework used to define ultra‑processed foods.

Health risk association claims (the dominant narrative)

Higher ultra‑processed food intake is linked with obesity/weight gain.
Linked with cardiometabolic outcomes: heart disease/cardiovascular disease, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome.
Linked with cancer risk (often framed as “increased overall cancer risk” or specific sites like digestive system, breast, colon).
Linked with mental/cognitive outcomes: depression/anxiety/mood disorders, cognitive decline, dementia, “declining mental health.”
Linked with digestive harms, gut inflammation, and microbiome disruption.

Mechanism claims (how harms supposedly happen)

Additives (especially emulsifiers/artificial sweeteners/preservatives) damage gut barrier function, alter the microbiome, inflame the intestinal lining, or erode the mucus layer.
Ultra‑processed foods “hijack” appetite and disrupt hunger/fullness cues, leading people to eat more (sometimes framed as overeating even when calories are comparable).
Claims about longer-term systemic effects: increased inflammation, “faster aging,” hormonal disruption.

Prevalence/scale claims (often numeric, frequently uncited in-post)

“50–70% of supermarket foods are ultra‑processed.”
“60% of the average diet is ultra‑processed (or industrial substances).”
“73% of the modern food supply is ultra‑processed.”
Ultra‑processed foods contribute “25%–60% of daily calories” in many populations.

Policy/regulation claims

Regulators are reviewing ultra‑processed foods for adverse health effects.
Countries are debating front‑of‑package warning labels, sugar taxes, and processing-based classification systems in response to ultra‑processed food concerns.

A key pattern in this discourse: many posts present broad health-impact claims confidently, but often without naming the specific study/review behind the number (e.g., “12 conditions,” “32 harmful effects,” “73% of food supply”). Where sources were available in the enrichment step, support was mixed and frequently indirect (definitions were easier to substantiate than precise numeric prevalence or specific biological mechanisms).
